# Carousel Framework — Slide Structures

> Pick a structure. Then pick an angle. Then write. Without a structure, every carousel becomes a bullet list.

The default range is **7-10 slides**. Anything shorter feels thin; anything longer loses people on slide 6.

This document defines:

1. The **default structure** — works 80% of the time.
2. **Seven alternative structures** — for specific situations.
3. **Slide roles** — the vocabulary used to describe what each slide is doing.
4. **Spacing principles** — how to pace tension across the carousel.

---

## Slide roles

Every slide plays exactly one role. Here's the vocabulary:

| Role | Job |
|---|---|
| **Hook** | Stop the scroll. Make slide 2 unavoidable. |
| **Problem** | Name the pain. Make the reader feel it. |
| **Mistake** | Show the wrong default behavior. |
| **Reframe** | Replace the old belief with a new one. |
| **Mechanism** | Explain *why* the new belief works. |
| **Example** | Make it concrete. Show, don't tell. |
| **Proof** | Numbers, quotes, screenshots, receipts. |
| **Comparison** | Side-by-side of two states or options. |
| **Step** | One actionable move (used in workflow / checklist structures). |
| **Trade-off** | Honestly name what you give up. |
| **Application** | "Here's how to apply this on Monday." |
| **Generalization** | The principle behind the specific. |
| **CTA** | The single action you want the reader to take. |
| **Bonus** | Optional. A bonus slide before CTA. Use sparingly. |

If you can't name the role, kill the slide.

## Default structure (8 slides)

The workhorse. Use this when the user gives you a topic and no special instructions.

```
1. Hook
2. Problem
3. Mistake (the common wrong move)
4. Reframe (new belief)
5. Mechanism (why the new belief works)
6. Example (or proof)
7. Application (how to apply on Monday)
8. CTA
```

**Why this works:** it follows the natural arc of *attention → tension → release → action*. Slide 1 makes you stop. Slides 2-3 build pressure. Slide 4 releases it with a new frame. Slide 5 earns the frame. Slide 6 grounds it. Slide 7 makes it usable. Slide 8 converts.

**Pacing tip:** slides 2-3 should feel like a pinch. Slides 4-5 should feel like a door opening.

## Alternative 1 — Launch (10 slides)

For product / feature / version launches.

```
1.  Hook (the headline, not the feature)
2.  Why now (the shift in the world that makes this needed)
3.  The old way (what people do today)
4.  The cost of the old way
5.  Introducing [PRODUCT/FEATURE]
6.  How it works (mechanism)
7.  What it replaces
8.  Use case 1
9.  Use case 2
10. CTA (try, join waitlist, read docs)
```

**Why this works:** the launch is framed as a *response to a shift*, not as "look at our cool thing." The reader earns the launch by understanding the why first.

**Watch out for:** burying the launch on slide 5. If your hook is "we built X," slide 5 is too late. Tease the launch on slide 1, deliver on slide 5.

---

## Alternative 2 — Educational (9 slides)

For teaching a concept, framework, or method.

```
1. Hook (the question or claim)
2. Why this matters (the cost of not knowing it)
3. The concept named
4. Component 1 (with example)
5. Component 2 (with example)
6. Component 3 (with example)
7. How to put it together
8. Where it breaks (be honest)
9. CTA (full guide, template, course)
```

**Why this works:** the audience for educational content wants to *understand*, not be sold. The honest "where it breaks" slide is the trust-builder.

**Watch out for:** dumping definitions. Each component slide must show the component in action, not just define it.

---

## Alternative 3 — Comparison (8 slides)

For "X vs Y" or "old way vs new way" posts.

```
1. Hook (the comparison framed)
2. The dimensions that matter
3. Option A overview
4. Option B overview
5. Where A wins (with use case)
6. Where B wins (with use case)
7. The recommendation by use case
8. CTA
```

**Why this works:** even-handed comparisons buy trust. People use them to make decisions and credit you for the clarity.

**Watch out for:** rigging the comparison. If A "wins" on every dimension, the post is an ad and the audience knows it.

---

## Alternative 4 — Benchmark / Proof (9 slides)

For data-driven posts with real numbers.

```
1. Hook (what was tested, sample size)
2. Methodology in plain language
3. The headline result
4. Breakdown by segment 1
5. Breakdown by segment 2
6. The surprise
7. The implication
8. Limitations / what we didn't measure
9. CTA (full report, dataset, methodology)
```

**Why this works:** data-savvy audiences crave methodology and limitations. Showing your work is the unlock.

**Watch out for:** numbers without context. A bar chart without sample size or methodology gets dismissed.

---

## Alternative 5 — Thought Leadership (8 slides)

For founder / executive / category-defining takes.

```
1. Hook (the strong opinion, stated plainly)
2. The status quo position
3. Why the status quo formed (steelman it)
4. Where the status quo breaks
5. The new position, in detail
6. Evidence / pattern from your experience
7. The principle, generalized
8. CTA (DM, book, newsletter, follow)
```

**Why this works:** thought leadership requires earning the right to disagree. Slide 3 (steelman) is the trust-builder. Skipping it makes you look unserious.

**Watch out for:** opinion without mechanism. "I think X" without "here's why X is true" is just noise.

---

## Alternative 6 — Product-Led (10 slides)

For posts where [PRODUCT] is the protagonist.

```
1.  Hook (the workflow / outcome, not the product)
2.  The job to be done
3.  The default tools / workflow
4.  Why the default fails
5.  Enter [PRODUCT]
6.  Feature 1 (with workflow image)
7.  Feature 2 (with workflow image)
8.  Feature 3 (with workflow image)
9.  Concrete result (with proof or [PROOF_NEEDED])
10. CTA
```

**Why this works:** the product is positioned as a *workflow upgrade*, not a list of features. Each feature slide shows the feature *in use*.

**Watch out for:** feature soup. If you can't pick the 3 features that matter most, the post becomes a brochure.

---

## Alternative 7 — Project Announcement (8 slides)

For open-source releases, side projects, public artifacts.

```
1. Hook (what it is, in one line)
2. Why we built it (the problem)
3. What's in v1
4. How it works (one diagram or one workflow)
5. Who it's for
6. Who it's not for (be honest)
7. What's next on the roadmap
8. CTA (link, install, star, follow)
```

**Why this works:** the "who it's not for" slide is rare and powerful. It signals that the writer respects the reader's time and attention.

**Watch out for:** under-describing v1. If the reader can't tell what the project actually does after slide 4, the post fails.

---

## Slide-count playbook

- **6 slides** — only when the topic is genuinely thin and you're being honest about it. Rare.
- **7 slides** — fine for tight, focused topics.
- **8 slides** — the workhorse. Default to this.
- **9 slides** — fine for educational or benchmark content.
- **10 slides** — fine for launches and product-led posts. Above 10, completion drops.
- **11+ slides** — almost never. If you need more, split into a series.

## Pacing principles

Carousels have rhythm. Treat them like a song.

```
[Slide 1]  ████████████████████  — peak attention, peak claim
[Slide 2]  ████████████          — pinch (problem)
[Slide 3]  ███████████           — deeper pinch (mistake)
[Slide 4]  ████████████████████  — release (reframe)
[Slide 5]  ████████████████      — sustain (mechanism)
[Slide 6]  ████████████████      — concretize (example/proof)
[Slide 7]  █████████████████     — empower (application)
[Slide 8]  ██████████████████    — call to act (CTA)
```

Three rules:

1. **Never put two low-energy slides back-to-back.** A "problem" slide followed by another "problem" slide kills momentum.
2. **Every slide must answer the question raised by the previous slide.** If slide 4 doesn't answer slide 3's pinch, the reader bounces.
3. **The CTA energy must match the post energy.** A high-stakes contrarian post can't end with "Hope this helps!"

---

## Hook → CTA alignment

The CTA must be a logical extension of the hook. If the hook is "Stop doing X," the CTA is something like "Comment your X" or "Share what you'll replace X with." If the hook is "I tested 50 tools," the CTA is "Drop your tool in the comments" or "Get the full spreadsheet."

Misaligned hook and CTA is the most common reason a "good" carousel underperforms.

## Slide template

For every slide, regardless of structure, write:

```
## Slide N — [Role]
- Role: [the role from the table above]
- Headline: [the line that goes on the slide, in plain text]
- Support text: [optional second line, max 8 words]
- Visual concept: [what the image shows, in one sentence]
- Image prompt: [a Midjourney/Flux/ChatGPT-Images-ready prompt]
- Layout instruction: [where headline sits, accent color, typography note]
```

Do not skip fields. If a slide doesn't need support text, write "—". The structure is the value.
