# Content System — 17 Carousel Angles

> The angle is the lever. Pick the wrong angle and even great copy falls flat. Pick the right angle and a mediocre execution still ships saves.

This document is a working library. Use it like a chef uses a spice rack: you don't pour everything in, you pick what fits the dish.

For every angle:

- **When to use** — the conditions where this angle outperforms.
- **Why it works** — the psychological lever.
- **Hook patterns** — fill-in-the-blank lines using the system's placeholders.
- **Slide structure** — the spine of a carousel built on this angle.
- **Watch out for** — the failure mode that makes this angle look like AI slop.

Placeholders used: `[BRAND]`, `[PRODUCT]`, `[CATEGORY]`, `[AUDIENCE]`, `[PROBLEM]`, `[SOLUTION]`, `[OUTCOME]`, `[PROOF]`, `[COMPETITOR]`, `[COMMON_BELIEF]`, `[NEW_BELIEF]`, `[METRIC]`, `[NUMBER]`, `[TIMEFRAME]`.

## 1. Contrarian

> "Everyone says X. Here's why X is wrong."

**When to use:** when the [CATEGORY] has a default belief that the [AUDIENCE] has stopped questioning. When you have a credible reason to disagree.

**Why it works:** novelty + identity. People share contrarian takes to signal they think for themselves.

**Hook patterns:**
- "Most [AUDIENCE] still believe [COMMON_BELIEF]. That's why they're stuck at [PROBLEM]."
- "Stop optimizing [X]. The actual lever is [Y]."
- "[COMMON_BELIEF] is the most expensive lie in [CATEGORY]."
- "If you're still doing [DEFAULT_BEHAVIOR] in [CURRENT_YEAR], you're paying a tax you can't see."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the consensus.
2. Show the cost of the consensus.
3. Reveal the contrarian belief.
4. Mechanism — why the contrarian belief is true.
5. Proof or pattern — where you've seen it work.
6. How to apply — the small move that flips it.
7. Reframe the consensus.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** being contrarian without an argument. Disagreeing with consensus is not a position; it's a pose. You need a *mechanism* in slide 4.

## 2. Mistake

> "I (or you) made this mistake. Here's what I learned."

**When to use:** when the [AUDIENCE] is making the same mistake you used to make. When humility earns trust.

**Why it works:** confession + utility. People lower their guard around someone admitting failure.

**Hook patterns:**
- "I lost [TIMEFRAME] of my life to [MISTAKE]. Don't be me."
- "The biggest [CATEGORY] mistake I see [AUDIENCE] make in their first year."
- "I used to think [OLD_BELIEF]. Then [PROBLEM] happened."
- "Three quiet mistakes that cost [AUDIENCE] [METRIC]."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the mistake plainly.
2. The setup — what made the mistake feel right at the time.
3. The cost — what it actually cost (specific).
4. The realization — the moment it cracked.
5. The new approach — what you do now.
6. The reason it works — the underlying mechanism.
7. The lesson, generalized — when this applies.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** humblebragging. "My biggest mistake was working too hard" is a mistake-shaped flex and the audience smells it.

---

## 3. Hidden Feature

> "There's a feature/move/lever in [PRODUCT or CATEGORY] that nobody is using. Here's how to use it."

**When to use:** when [PRODUCT] has a powerful feature that's underused. When [CATEGORY] has a low-effort, high-leverage move that's underrated.

**Why it works:** insider knowledge + immediate utility. People save posts that make them feel like they got the cheat code.

**Hook patterns:**
- "There's a [FEATURE] inside [PRODUCT] that 90% of [AUDIENCE] never open."
- "The cheapest way to fix [PROBLEM] is hidden three menus deep."
- "Most [AUDIENCE] never use [FEATURE]. The 5% who do save [TIMEFRAME] per week."
- "[PRODUCT] has a [FEATURE] mode. It quietly replaces [TOOL/PROCESS]."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the hidden feature.
2. The pain it solves.
3. Where to find it (with implied screenshot/visual).
4. How it works in 30 seconds.
5. A concrete use case.
6. The result.
7. Why nobody talks about it.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** clickbait. If the "hidden feature" is a setting toggle anyone could find in 5 seconds, the audience will resent the buildup.

---

## 4. Before/After

> "Here's the before. Here's the after. Here's what changed."

**When to use:** when you have a clear, visualizable transformation. When the [OUTCOME] is dramatic enough to be its own story.

**Why it works:** transformation is the oldest narrative. The brain is wired to compare states.

**Hook patterns:**
- "I rebuilt [PROCESS/SYSTEM] in 7 days. The before vs after."
- "What [METRIC] looked like before [SOLUTION]. And after."
- "[AUDIENCE]'s workflow at month 0 vs month 6."
- "The same [INPUT], processed by [OLD_WAY] and [NEW_WAY]. Side by side."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — set the timeline (before/after, day 1/day 30, etc).
2. Before state — what it looked like, in detail.
3. The trigger — what made you change.
4. The intervention — what you did.
5. After state — what it looks like now.
6. The metric that changed.
7. What you'd do differently.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** dishonest "after." If the after isn't real or isn't credible, the post collapses. Use [PROOF_NEEDED] aggressively.

---

## 5. Comparison

> "[X] vs [Y]. Here's how they differ. Here's which one fits when."

**When to use:** when [AUDIENCE] is actively choosing between two options. When you can be even-handed (this matters; one-sided comparisons read as ads).

**Why it works:** it answers a question the audience is already asking. It positions you as the trusted referee.

**Hook patterns:**
- "[A] vs [B]: which one wins for [USE_CASE]?"
- "I ran [A] and [B] head-to-head for [TIMEFRAME]. The result surprised me."
- "Stop comparing [A] and [B] on price. Compare them on [DIMENSION]."
- "When to pick [A]. When to pick [B]. When to pick neither."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the comparison.
2. The dimensions that actually matter.
3. [A] strengths and weaknesses.
4. [B] strengths and weaknesses.
5. The dimension where [A] wins.
6. The dimension where [B] wins.
7. The recommendation by use case.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** straw-manning the competitor. If your "comparison" is rigged, your audience will lose trust permanently. Be fair on purpose.

---

## 6. Myth vs Reality

> "[CATEGORY] myth: X. Reality: Y."

**When to use:** when there are persistent myths in your category. Especially powerful for technical, regulated, or complex categories.

**Why it works:** clarity is rare. People share posts that feel like the first honest answer they've heard.

**Hook patterns:**
- "5 myths about [CATEGORY] that quietly cost [AUDIENCE] [METRIC]."
- "Myth: [COMMON_BELIEF]. Reality: [NEW_BELIEF]."
- "I used to believe [LIST OF MYTHS]. Then I shipped [N] [PROJECT_TYPE]s."
- "If your [CATEGORY] strategy still relies on [MYTH], read this before your next [DECISION_POINT]."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — list the myths you'll bust.
2-6. One slide per myth: name it, then dismantle it.
7. The principle that ties them together.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** strawman myths. If nobody actually believes the "myth," you're shadowboxing.

---

## 7. Benchmark / Proof

> "We tested [N] [THINGS]. Here's the data."

**When to use:** when you have real, defensible numbers. When [AUDIENCE] is data-literate and skeptical of hand-wavy claims.

**Why it works:** data is currency. A single credible chart can carry an entire post.

**Hook patterns:**
- "We benchmarked [N] [TOOLS/MODELS/METHODS] across [DIMENSIONS]. Full results."
- "[N] hours of [ACTIVITY], compressed into one chart."
- "What [METRIC] actually looks like when you measure it across [SAMPLE_SIZE]."
- "The numbers nobody publishes about [CATEGORY]."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — what was tested, sample size, what we measured.
2. Methodology in plain language.
3. The headline result.
4. The surprise — what didn't behave as expected.
5. The breakdown by segment.
6. The implication — what this means for [AUDIENCE].
7. Limitations / what we didn't measure.
8. CTA — link to full data, methodology, or report.

**Watch out for:** numbers without methodology. The audience for this angle is the most skeptical; they'll smell hand-waving immediately. If the methodology is weak, change angles.

## 8. Build in Public

> "Here's what we shipped this week. Here's what we learned."

**When to use:** when you want to compound trust over time. When the audience cares about the journey, not just the destination.

**Why it works:** parasocial trust. Watching someone build is more intimate than reading their case study.

**Hook patterns:**
- "Week [N] of building [PRODUCT]. Here's what broke and what shipped."
- "I rewrote [SYSTEM] from scratch this week. Receipts inside."
- "What it actually took to ship [FEATURE] in [TIMEFRAME]."
- "Building [PRODUCT] in public. Month [N] update."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — the timeframe and the headline.
2. What we shipped.
3. What broke.
4. The lesson we paid for.
5. What's coming next.
6. The metric we're watching.
7. An open question for the audience.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** turning every update into a launch ad. Build-in-public works when the writer is honest about *not* having it figured out.

---

## 9. Founder Perspective

> "I built [BRAND]. Here's what nobody tells you."

**When to use:** when the founder is a credible voice. When [AUDIENCE] cares about decisions, not just features.

**Why it works:** authority + intimacy. People follow founders to learn how to think, not just what to buy.

**Hook patterns:**
- "I've shipped [PRODUCT] for [TIMEFRAME]. Here's the unpopular take."
- "What I'd tell my younger founder self before starting [BRAND]."
- "The hardest [DECISION_TYPE] I've made building [BRAND]."
- "Why I killed [FEATURE] even though customers asked for it."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — claim a strong, specific opinion.
2. The context — when this opinion formed.
3. The opposing view — what most people would say.
4. The reasoning — why you disagree.
5. The cost — what this opinion has cost you.
6. The result — what it's bought you.
7. The principle, generalized.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** "advice from the cave" energy. If the founder hasn't shipped, the angle inverts and looks like LARP.

---

## 10. Technical Breakdown

> "Here's how [SYSTEM/FEATURE] actually works under the hood."

**When to use:** when [AUDIENCE] is technical. When the topic rewards depth.

**Why it works:** signal of expertise. People save technical breakdowns to reference later.

**Hook patterns:**
- "How [PRODUCT] handles [HARD_PROBLEM] under the hood."
- "The architecture I'd reach for if I were rebuilding [SYSTEM] today."
- "[N] design decisions that quietly determine whether [PRODUCT] scales."
- "What happens when you click [BUTTON] in [PRODUCT] (annotated)."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the system / problem / decision.
2. The naive approach.
3. Why the naive approach fails.
4. The actual approach.
5. The trade-offs.
6. The edge case worth knowing.
7. The general principle.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** writing for yourself. If a senior in the field can't follow the breakdown without prior context, the post is too compressed.

---

## 11. Workflow Reveal

> "Here's exactly how I do [TASK] in [TIMEFRAME]."

**When to use:** when you have a process worth showing. When [AUDIENCE] is hungry for replicable workflows.

**Why it works:** template energy. The audience wants something they can copy on Monday.

**Hook patterns:**
- "My exact workflow for [TASK] in [TIMEFRAME]."
- "How [AUDIENCE] can ship [DELIVERABLE] in [N] hours instead of [N] days."
- "The 4-step process I use to [TASK] without [PAIN_POINT]."
- "My [TOOL_STACK] for [TASK]. With the order it runs in."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the task and the timeframe.
2. Step 1 — input + tool + outcome.
3. Step 2 — input + tool + outcome.
4. Step 3 — input + tool + outcome.
5. Step 4 — input + tool + outcome.
6. The bottleneck this workflow removes.
7. Where it breaks (be honest).
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** workflows that only work for the writer. Generalize the steps before publishing.

---

## 12. Checklist

> "[N] things to check before [DECISION/EVENT]."

**When to use:** when [AUDIENCE] is about to make a decision and is anxious. When the topic has a finite list of failure modes.

**Why it works:** checklists reduce cognitive load. They get saved.

**Hook patterns:**
- "Before you ship [FEATURE/PRODUCT/CONTENT], run this checklist."
- "[N] questions to ask before signing with [VENDOR_TYPE]."
- "The 7-point pre-flight check for [TASK]."
- "If your [PROCESS] doesn't pass these [N] checks, fix it before [EVENT]."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the decision and the cost of getting it wrong.
2-7. One slide per check (or 2 checks per slide if shorter).
8. The principle behind the checklist + CTA.

**Watch out for:** generic checklists. "Have you defined your audience?" is not a check; it's a vibe. Each item should be specific enough that a reader can mark it done or not done.

---

## 13. Opinionated Ranking

> "I ranked [N] [THINGS]. Here's the list."

**When to use:** when [AUDIENCE] argues about rankings in your category. When you can defend your top and bottom picks.

**Why it works:** rankings are inherently shareable. People comment to defend their favorite.

**Hook patterns:**
- "I ranked [N] [TOOLS/METHODS/COMPANIES] for [USE_CASE]. Fight me."
- "The honest tier list of [CATEGORY] in [CURRENT_YEAR]."
- "Top [N] [THINGS] for [AUDIENCE]. Ordered."
- "From best to worst: [N] [THINGS] I've used."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — what's being ranked, on what dimension.
2. Tier S / #1 — what makes it #1.
3-7. Subsequent tiers.
8. The wildcard — the one that didn't make the cut and why + CTA.

**Watch out for:** rankings without rationale. "I just like X better" is not defensible. Each placement needs a one-line reason.

## 14. "You're Using X Wrong"

> "Most [AUDIENCE] use [PRODUCT/METHOD] like this. They should use it like this instead."

**When to use:** when there's a default usage pattern that's measurably suboptimal. When you can show a better way without insulting the reader.

**Why it works:** identity threat + relief. The reader hates feeling wrong but loves feeling fixed.

**Hook patterns:**
- "You're using [PRODUCT] wrong. Here's the workflow it was designed for."
- "Most [AUDIENCE] use [TOOL] as a [WRONG_THING]. It's actually a [RIGHT_THING]."
- "The expensive way [AUDIENCE] use [PRODUCT]. And the cheap way that works better."
- "If you're [DEFAULT_USAGE], you're paying for [N]% of features you'll never see."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — accuse the default usage.
2. The default usage in detail.
3. Why it became the default.
4. Where it breaks down.
5. The correct usage.
6. The mechanism that makes it work.
7. The result.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** condescension. The line between "you're using it wrong" and "you're an idiot" is exactly one word. Stay on the right side.

---

## 15. "Stop Doing X"

> "Stop [BEHAVIOR]. Do [BETTER_BEHAVIOR] instead."

**When to use:** when [AUDIENCE] has a specific bad habit. When the alternative is concrete.

**Why it works:** prescriptive clarity. People are tired of "do more of this." They want "stop doing that."

**Hook patterns:**
- "Stop [DEFAULT_BEHAVIOR]. It's costing you [METRIC]."
- "[N] habits [AUDIENCE] should kill in [CURRENT_YEAR]."
- "Stop doing [TASK] manually. Here's what to automate instead."
- "If you're still [BEHAVIOR], stop today. Here's why."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — the behavior to kill.
2. Why people do it.
3. What it costs.
4. The replacement.
5. Why the replacement is harder (be honest).
6. Why it's worth it anyway.
7. The first 24-hour move.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** scolding. "Stop doing X" works when paired with empathy for *why* people do X.

---

## 16. "Nobody Tells You This About X"

> "Nobody talks about [TRUTH] in [CATEGORY]. Here it is."

**When to use:** when you have an insight that's almost-but-not-quite obvious. When the [AUDIENCE] is ready for a frame they haven't seen.

**Why it works:** the feeling of being let in. Readers save these posts because they feel like privileged information.

**Hook patterns:**
- "Nobody talks about [TRUTH] in [CATEGORY]. Here's why it matters."
- "The unspoken rule of [CATEGORY] that changes everything."
- "[N] things [SUCCESSFUL_GROUP] do that nobody writes about."
- "What [CATEGORY] insiders know that beginners miss."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — name the unspoken truth.
2. The polite version that gets said.
3. The honest version (your insight).
4. Why nobody says it.
5. The implication.
6. The move it unlocks.
7. The principle, generalized.
8. CTA.

**Watch out for:** trivia disguised as insight. If "nobody tells you this" turns out to be common knowledge, the post collapses.

---

## 17. "I Tested X So You Don't Have To"

> "I spent [TIMEFRAME] / [MONEY] testing [N] [THINGS]. Here's what I found."

**When to use:** when you've done real, expensive research. When [AUDIENCE] would have to do the same work themselves.

**Why it works:** debt collection. The audience feels grateful that someone else paid the cost.

**Hook patterns:**
- "I tested [N] [TOOLS] for [TASK] so you don't have to."
- "I spent $[AMOUNT] / [TIMEFRAME] running [TEST]. Here's what worked."
- "[N] [THINGS] tested. [M] survived. Here's the survivor list."
- "I built the same [PROJECT_TYPE] [N] ways to find the fastest one."

**Slide structure:**
1. Hook — what you tested, sample size, what you spent.
2. The methodology in plain language.
3. The biggest surprise.
4. The clear winner.
5. The clear loser.
6. The middle ground (and when to pick it).
7. The honest caveat.
8. CTA — full results / template / repo.

**Watch out for:** results without methodology. This angle's whole credibility comes from showing your work.

## How to pick an angle

A 3-step decision flow:

1. **What's your strongest signal?**
   - Hard data → angles 7, 17 (Benchmark / Tested).
   - Strong opinion → angles 1, 9, 14, 15, 16 (Contrarian / Founder / Wrong / Stop / Nobody Tells).
   - Personal story → angles 2, 8, 9 (Mistake / Build in Public / Founder).
   - Useful process → angles 3, 11, 12 (Hidden Feature / Workflow / Checklist).
   - Comparison data → angles 5, 13 (Comparison / Ranking).
   - Educational depth → angles 6, 10 (Myth vs Reality / Technical Breakdown).
   - Transformation → angle 4 (Before/After).

2. **What does the audience need?**
   - Validation → angles 9, 16.
   - Permission → angles 1, 14, 15.
   - A template → angles 3, 11, 12.
   - A decision → angles 5, 13, 17.
   - A new mental model → angles 6, 10.

3. **What does the platform reward?**
   - LinkedIn → 9, 11, 14, 15, 16 reward you with comments and DMs.
   - Instagram → 4, 7, 13, 17 reward you with saves and shares.
   - X (Twitter) → 1, 9, 14, 15 reward you with quote-tweets.
   - TikTok carousels → 4, 7, 11, 17 reward you with completion rate.

If you're stuck, default to **Mistake (2)** for warmth or **Contrarian (1)** for reach. Both are durable.

## Mixing angles

The strongest carousels often combine two angles. Example combinations:

- **Contrarian + Benchmark** — "Everyone says X. We tested it. They're wrong."
- **Mistake + Workflow** — "I used to do it this way. Here's the workflow that fixed it."
- **Founder + Build in Public** — "Here's the call I made this quarter and what happened."
- **Hidden Feature + Workflow** — "There's a feature in [PRODUCT] that replaces a 4-step workflow."
- **Myth vs Reality + Technical Breakdown** — "Myth says X scales. Here's what actually breaks."

When mixing, name both angles in the Strategic Angle section of the output. Don't blend them silently.
