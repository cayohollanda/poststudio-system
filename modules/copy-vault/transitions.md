# Transitions — Pattern Library

> Slide-to-slide transitions. Each slide must answer the question raised by the previous one. Transitions are the bridge.

---

## Implicit transitions (preferred)

Most slide-to-slide moves should *imply* the connection through the slide's role. The reader feels the bridge without being told.

```
Slide 2 (problem): "At week 6, my mornings stopped surviving past 10am."
Slide 3 (mistake): "I kept installing tools to fix the symptoms."
```

The transition is the role sequence: problem → mistake. No transition word required.

---

## Explicit transitions (use sparingly)

When the bridge is non-obvious, signal it:

### Cause-to-effect
```
Slide 4 ends: "...the more interruptions, the less deep work."
Slide 5 opens: "So we tried something structural."
```

### Reframe-to-mechanism
```
Slide 4: "The chat is the problem."
Slide 5 opens with: "Here's why the chat fails for focus."
```

### Argument-to-proof
```
Slide 5 ends: "Senior operators have fewer surfaces, not more discipline."
Slide 6 opens: "Here's what changed when we tested that."
```

### Application-to-CTA
```
Slide 7: "Three categories. One question each."
Slide 8 (CTA): "Save this for the next time you're about to install."
```

---

## Banned transition words / phrases

These mark a post as written by an AI / corporate writer:

- "So..."
- "And here's the thing..."
- "Now, the next part..."
- "Let me explain..."
- "But wait, there's more..."
- "On top of that..."
- "Furthermore..."
- "In addition..."
- "Moreover..."
- "What's interesting is..."

Replace with implicit bridges or specific reframings.

---

## Transition rhythm

Carousels have rhythm. Treat transitions like a song:

- **Pinch** (slides 2-3): build pressure.
- **Release** (slide 4): pop the tension.
- **Sustain** (slides 5-6): hold the new frame.
- **Activation** (slide 7): show the reader the move.
- **Punch** (slide 8): close on the CTA.

If transitions don't follow this rhythm, the carousel feels mushy.

---

## Test for a clean transition

Read slide N out loud. Then read slide N+1 out loud. Ask:

- Does N+1 *answer* N?
- Does N+1 *escalate* N?
- Does N+1 *resolve* N?

If N+1 just *adds* to N, the transition is weak; combine the two slides or kill one.

---

## When you need a hard transition

If the carousel pivots dramatically (e.g. from problem to product), the transition slide can be its own slide:

```
Slide 4: "What we built instead."
```

Brief. Strong. One sentence. The reader pauses, then keeps swiping.

Use this sparingly — once per carousel max.
