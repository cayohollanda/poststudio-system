# Audience — `lumen`

> Senior operators inside scaling SaaS companies who are quietly losing their week to interruption tax — and have stopped believing that "more AI" is the answer.

---

## Primary audience

- **Role:** Senior PMs, engineering leads, design leads, and founders/COOs at 50-500 person SaaS companies.
- **Situation:** Their calendar has filled with 30-minute meetings. Their Slack DMs never close. They installed five "AI productivity" tools in the last quarter and feel measurably *worse*. They've started skipping standups to recover focus.
- **Where they are in the buying journey:** Mostly **problem-aware** drifting into **solution-aware**. They know the chat-tool model isn't working but haven't named the alternative yet.

### Their #1 pain
> "I keep starting my mornings with a plan and ending them confused. By 10am I'm reactive again."

### Their #1 desire
> Two uninterrupted blocks of deep work per day, without having to fight for them every morning.

### What they tried before
- **Calendar-blocking tools** (Reclaim, Clockwise) — helps with surface-level scheduling but doesn't reduce the underlying decision load.
- **AI chat tools** (general-purpose assistants) — useful for one-off tasks; toxic for sustained focus because the interaction is interruptive by design.
- **Note-taking + second-brain systems** (Notion, Obsidian, Roam) — increased capture, did nothing for execution.
- **Pure willpower** ("I'll just be more disciplined") — fails within 2-3 weeks.

### Beliefs they hold that are *getting in their way*
- "If I just get the right AI tool, I'll get faster." → False premise. The interaction model is the bottleneck, not the model quality.
- "Inbox-zero / calendar-tetris discipline is the path to deep work." → False. It's a coping mechanism, not a strategy.
- "Senior operators just have more focus than I do." → False. They have *fewer interruption surfaces*, not more willpower.

### Beliefs they hold that we *agree with*
- Deep work is a finite, depleting resource — not a habit you can will into existence.
- Most "AI features" inside existing tools are noise, not leverage.
- The actual senior-operator advantage is *what they don't engage with*, not *what they engage with faster*.

### Where they hang out online
- LinkedIn — comment threads under operator-y voices and PM/eng-leadership writers.
- Specific Substacks: founder ops, engineering management, async work culture.
- Closed Slack/Discord communities for PM-operators and founder-operators.
- Hacker News — read mode, rarely comment.
- Twitter/X — they lurk on a few engineering-leader and founder accounts.

### What they read / watch / listen to
- *Lenny's Newsletter* (PM/operator)
- *The Pragmatic Engineer* (eng leadership)
- *Stratechery* (strategy)
- *Acquired* podcast (long-form business)
- *Founders* podcast (operator psychology)
- The occasional *New York Times Magazine* / *The Atlantic* deep-read on attention/work

## Secondary audiences

### Segment 2 — Founder-operators at seed/Series A startups
- **Role:** Solo or co-founder, technical or non-technical, leading a 5-30 person team.
- **Situation:** Wears five hats. Their week is fully reactive. Has tried Notion, ChatGPT, and a focus app — none stuck.
- **Why they matter:** Highest urgency, fastest to convert, but lowest LTV. Best for product-led growth and word-of-mouth.

### Segment 3 — Senior individual contributors at the IC track (staff+, principal engineers)
- **Role:** Staff engineer, principal designer, senior data scientist, etc.
- **Situation:** Has won the right to do deep work in theory; in practice, the calendar still owns them.
- **Why they matter:** Strong evangelists; vocal on social. They legitimize the brand inside their company before it spreads to leadership.

---

## Non-audience (explicit)

- **Customer support / sales / account management roles.** Their work is fundamentally interruption-driven. Lumen is not for them; positioning otherwise would dilute the brand.
- **Students and early-career individual contributors.** They don't yet have the calendar autonomy that makes Lumen valuable.
- **Casual users looking for a Pomodoro app.** Lumen is not a focus timer. We will explicitly say "this is not for you."
- **Heavy email users in regulated industries (legal, compliance, healthcare admin).** Their workflow is constraint-bound in ways Lumen does not solve.

---

## How they speak

- **Vocabulary they actually use:** "interrupt budget," "context switch tax," "deep work," "shipping cadence," "reactive week," "calendar tetris," "the meeting that should have been a doc," "leverage points."
- **Vocabulary they hate:** "synergy," "supercharge," "transform your workflow," "game-changing," "10x your productivity," "AI-powered" (generic), "unleash your potential," "rockstar," "ninja," "guru."
- **Reading level / formality:** Mid-formal. They read books. They use full sentences. They tolerate em dashes but prefer prose to bullet-spam in long-form.
- **Lowercase or proper case in their own writing:** Mixed. Many lean lowercase-friendly on social, fully proper case at work.
- **Are they ironic / earnest / both?** **Both.** They like dry humor and earnest frameworks in the same post. They distrust pure earnestness ("hustle") and pure irony ("everything is a joke").

## Sample personas

### Persona A — "The recently-promoted PM lead"
Maya, 31, PM lead at a 200-person Series C SaaS. Three months into a new role managing four PMs. Her calendar got 30% denser the day she got the title. She's still trying to ship her own product strategy work in the evenings because mornings are gone to 1:1s and standups. She follows Lenny's Newsletter, lurks on PM Twitter, and has installed and uninstalled three AI tools this quarter. She's starting to suspect "more AI" is making it worse, but doesn't have language for it yet. Lumen lands when she sees a post that names her exact week.

### Persona B — "The founder-operator in Series-A grind"
Kenji, 36, founder/CEO of a 22-person fintech infrastructure startup. Pre-Series-B. Spends his week toggling between fundraising calls, customer escalations, and ad-hoc engineering decisions. His mornings used to be deep-work; for six months, they haven't been. He's bought every productivity book, knows the literature, but the *system* keeps slipping. He's not looking for tips — he's looking for someone who already lost this fight and figured out the structural fix.

### Persona C — "The staff engineer who refuses to manage"
Priya, 38, staff engineer at a 400-person infrastructure company. Has explicitly opted out of management. Her stated job is hard technical problems; her actual job has 22 hours of meetings per week. She's vocal on Twitter/X about the "interruption tax." She'd love a tool that gave her morning back without requiring her to install yet another browser extension. She'll evangelize Lumen if it works — and roast it publicly if it doesn't.
