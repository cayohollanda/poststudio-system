# Content Principles

> The philosophy under PostStudio System. If you only have time to read one doc before generating, this is it.

These principles are not "tips." They are the priors the whole system is built on. When the rules in `system/` and the principles here disagree (rare), the principles win. When the rules go silent on something, derive the answer from the principles.

---

## 1. Stop the scroll, then earn the swipe

The first slide is 80% of the post's job. The next 7 slides exist only because the first one earned the right.

A good first slide does three things in 0.5 seconds:

1. Pattern interrupt (visual or copy).
2. Promise (the reader knows what they'll get).
3. Tension (something is at stake if they keep scrolling).

If your first slide does only one or two, the carousel collapses no matter how good slides 2-8 are.

---

## 2. Specificity beats cleverness

"Better than the rest" — dead.
"3 lessons from building a startup" — dead.
"How [specific person] [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe] without [specific pain]" — alive.

Specificity earns trust. Cleverness asks for it. Pick specificity every time.

When you can't be specific, ask the user for inputs. If they don't provide, mark `[PROOF_NEEDED]`. Never inflate vagueness with adjectives.

---

## 3. Tension is the whole game

A carousel without tension is a brochure.

Find the fault line:

- Old belief vs new belief.
- Default behavior vs better behavior.
- Industry consensus vs uncomfortable truth.
- What the audience expects vs what's actually happening.
- What you wish were true vs what you've learned.

The first 3 slides build pressure. Slides 4-5 release it. The rest pay it off. If you can't name the tension in one sentence, you don't have a carousel — you have a list.

---

## 4. One idea per slide

Every slide carries exactly one idea, named in ≤4 words.

If a slide has two ideas, split it. If a slide has no idea, kill it. If you can't compress a slide's idea to 4 words, it's mushy.

A 7-slide post with 7 sharp ideas beats a 12-slide post with 12 fuzzy ones every time.

---

## 5. Show the mechanism

Conclusions are cheap. Mechanisms are expensive.

A weak post says: "X works."
A strong post says: "X works because [specific reason]."

The mechanism is the slide that earns the rest. Without it, your contrarian take is a vibe and your before/after is a coincidence. Every framework, opinion, or recommendation in a carousel needs at least one slide explaining *why* it's true.

---

## 6. Visual carries the meaning

A slide is not a text block on a colored rectangle. A slide is a frame: text + image + role.

The image isn't decoration. It's argument. A glass dome cracking says something about fragility. A dashboard fading into shadow says something about obsolescence. The image earns half the slide's weight.

If your slide reads exactly the same with the image removed, the image is decoration and you're posting glorified PowerPoint.

---

## 7. Respect the reader's time

Carousels are read by busy people on phones in moments of low attention. They:

- Don't read every word.
- Skim headlines first.
- Decide to swipe in 1-2 seconds per slide.
- Bounce on any slide that feels like work.

Build for skimming. Headlines should carry the argument from slide 1 to slide N. Body copy should reward stopping. Slides should never punish the reader for being fast.

---

## 8. Default to social-native voice

Carousels are not press releases. They are conversations.

Default voice:

- First person ("I" or "we") — never "the team" or "the brand."
- Active voice — never passive.
- Conversational rhythm — short sentences, mixed in with one longer one.
- Plain language — no jargon, no marketing-speak.
- Honest — including admitting what you don't know.
- Opinionated — taking a stance, not "balancing perspectives."

The Brand Pack can override defaults, but most brands lean *more* casual on social than they think.

---

## 9. Honest claims, defensible numbers

A great hook can build a brand. An invented number can break it.

Never write a number, customer, award, or quote that wasn't in the brief. Never round generously. Never imply scale you don't have. Never disparage a competitor by name without authorization.

If you don't have proof, mark `[PROOF_NEEDED]` and surface it. The audience for your best content is the most skeptical; they'll catch the fabrication, and the rebuilt trust is more expensive than the saved post.

---

## 10. The CTA is a single action

A carousel asks for one thing. Not three. Not "save and share and follow and DM me."

Pick the one ask that fits the angle:

- Save (utility content).
- Share (contrarian / cultural moment).
- Comment (opinion / ranking / question-bait).
- Click (data, downloads, longer reads).
- Try (product-led).
- DM (high-intent, narrow audience).
- Follow (founder / build-in-public).

The CTA's energy must match the post's energy. A serious founder essay can't end with "Hope this helps!" A playful meme can't end with a tactical PDF link.

---

## 11. The post lives in a system

Every carousel is part of a longer arc:

- It teaches the audience what to expect from the brand.
- It deposits proof into the Brand Pack for the next post.
- It builds a recognizable visual signature.
- It compounds with prior posts.

Don't post in isolation. Each output should leave the next one easier to make. Capture proof, refine the Brand Pack, save alternative hooks.

The system is the moat.

---

## 12. Reviewing is a separate job

The writer pass is generative. The reviewer pass is adversarial.

The same person/agent can play both roles, but never in the same response. Switch contexts. Use `prompts/critique-carousel.md`. Be willing to fail your own draft.

A self-approved draft is the most common cause of posts that look fine and underperform.

---

## 13. Don't post for yourself

The post is for the reader.

Your favorite hook is not necessarily their best hook. Your most clever metaphor might confuse them. Your in-joke is opaque to outsiders. Test against the audience, not against your own taste.

The PostStudio quality checklist exists exactly to catch the writer's bias.

---

## 14. Friction is fuel

Posts that get the most engagement are usually the ones the writer hesitated to ship.

If a draft feels safe — too balanced, too palatable, too "everyone will agree" — it probably won't reach anyone.

Sharpness travels. Smoothness vanishes.

Lean into the slight discomfort of taking a clear stance. The Brand Pack's `claims-allowed` and `claims-forbidden` lists exist to keep that sharpness on the right side of the line.

---

## 15. Ship, then learn

A perfect post that doesn't ship teaches you nothing.
A flawed post that ships teaches you something every time.

The system optimizes for ship rate first, then for ship quality. Don't let the third revision become the eighth. Hit the quality gate, surface remaining flags, and ship.

---

## How to apply these in practice

When you're stuck:

- "Is the hook specific?" — fix specificity (#2).
- "Does this have tension?" — find the fault line (#3).
- "Why is this slide here?" — apply one-idea-per-slide (#4).
- "Why does this work?" — add a mechanism slide (#5).
- "Does the image earn its space?" — apply visual-carries-meaning (#6).
- "Is this honest?" — apply honest-claims (#9).
- "What am I asking the reader to do?" — fix the CTA (#10).

These principles are the debugging vocabulary. When something feels off, name the principle that's being violated.
